7 Clauses That Quietly Catch Out Freelancers

The contract traps that cost creators & freelancers money — and the exact words to push back with.

C&E Clause & Effect · plain-English contract checks, London

Most freelancers sign contracts they haven't fully read — and the costly stuff is rarely on page one. Here are the seven clauses we flag most often, what they really mean, and what to ask for instead. Spot any of these in your next contract? Get it checked (free) at the link at the bottom.

1. Exclusivity

What it says: you can't work with competing clients/brands for a set period.

Why it bites: a 12-month exclusivity on a one-off £400 job can block thousands in other work — usually unpaid.

Ask for: cap it to 30 days, limit it to direct competitors only, or get paid extra for it.

2. Perpetual usage / IP rights

What it says: they can use, edit and repurpose your work “perpetually, worldwide, across all media.”

Why it bites: your face or work can run in paid ads forever, for the original fee only.

Ask for: cap usage to the named campaign and a set time (e.g. 6 months); charge a usage fee for anything beyond.

3. Unlimited revisions

What it says: you'll revise “until the client is satisfied.”

Why it bites: there's no end point — one job can swallow weeks for a fixed fee.

Ask for: a set number of revision rounds (e.g. 2), with extra rounds charged hourly.

4. Long / vague payment terms

What it says: payment “within 60–90 days,” or only “on completion.”

Why it bites: you're effectively lending the client money, and “completion” can be moved.

Ask for: 14–30 day terms, a deposit upfront (e.g. 50%), and late-payment interest.

5. One-sided termination

What it says: the client can end the contract any time; you can't.

Why it bites: they can walk after you've done the work — and you're still locked in.

Ask for: mutual termination rights and a kill fee if they cancel after you've started.

6. Indemnity & liability

What it says: you cover the client's losses if something goes wrong.

Why it bites: uncapped liability can be far bigger than the whole fee.

Ask for: cap your liability at the fee paid, and exclude indirect/consequential losses.

7. Non-disclosure that's too broad

What it says: you can't mention the work at all — sometimes forever.

Why it bites: you can't add it to your portfolio, which is how freelancers get the next job.

Ask for: the right to show the work in your portfolio, or a defined NDA time limit.
